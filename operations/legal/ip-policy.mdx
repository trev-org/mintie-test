When you're employed by Intuit, your work-product IP belongs to Intuit. This page explains what that means and what carve-outs exist.

## What's "work product"

Anything you create:

- During work hours
- On Intuit equipment
- Using Intuit confidential information
- That relates to Intuit's actual or anticipated business

Work product is owned by Intuit per your employment agreement.

## What's *yours*

The carve-out: things you create:

- On your own time
- On your own equipment
- Without using Intuit confidential information
- Not relating to Intuit's business

Side projects in unrelated domains: yours. A spreadsheet template for personal use: yours. Open-source contributions to projects unrelated to Intuit's work: yours, with the OSS contribution disclosure.

## Side projects — the disclosure rule

If you have a side project that **could** be ambiguous, disclose it via Workday → **IP Disclosure**. We:

- Confirm whether it's outside Intuit IP
- Get it on the record so there's no dispute later
- Do not impede unrelated personal work

Side projects that are competitive with Intuit's business require approval (and often won't be approved).

## Open-source contributions

We encourage open-source contributions:

- **Direct work-product OSS**: Intuit-owned, contributed under approved license
- **Personal OSS, unrelated to work**: yours, no disclosure needed
- **Personal OSS, related to work**: disclose via the IP form

For direct work-product OSS releases (open-sourcing an Intuit project), see the [open-source program](https://opensource.intuit.com).

## Patents

Intuit files patents on novel work product. If you contribute to an invention:

- You're listed as inventor on the application
- Patent rights belong to Intuit
- Inventors receive a **patent award** of $5,000 at filing, $10,000 at grant
- Multiple inventions count individually

## Trademarks

Intuit trademarks (including TurboTax, QuickBooks, Mint, Credit Karma, Mailchimp) have strict usage guidelines. Public-facing usage requires brand approval. Internal use is fine.

## Customer IP

Customer data is not Intuit IP — it's the customer's. Our license to use it is governed by our Terms of Service for that product. See [Privacy](/operations/legal/privacy-gdpr-ccpa).

## Things that get people in trouble

- Using customer data for personal projects
- Open-sourcing internal code without approval
- Working on a side project that competes with Intuit
- Sharing Intuit code with people outside Intuit (including in interviews — talk *about* what you built, don't show the code)
