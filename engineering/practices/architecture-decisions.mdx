We use **Architecture Decision Records (ADRs)** to capture significant technical choices. ADRs prevent the same arguments from being relitigated every six months.

## When to write an ADR

- A choice with multi-quarter consequences
- A choice that locks in technology, vendor, or major design
- A choice with disagreement on the team
- A choice you'd struggle to explain to a new hire in 12 months

You don't need an ADR for: framework version bumps, library swaps with no API change, or routine refactors.

## Where ADRs live

Inside the service repo at `docs/adr/NNNN-<title>.md`. Numbered sequentially. Markdown.

For cross-team ADRs ("we're standardizing on X across SBSE"), they live in `intuit-platform/adrs/`.

## Template

```markdown
# ADR-NNNN: <title>

- **Status**: Proposed | Accepted | Deprecated | Superseded by ADR-XYZ
- **Date**: 2026-05-04
- **Deciders**: @author, @tech-lead, @reviewer

## Context
What's the situation? What problem are we solving?

## Decision
What did we decide? Be specific.

## Alternatives considered
At least 2. For each: brief description, why not chosen.

## Consequences
- Positive: ...
- Negative: ...
- Neutral: ...

## Follow-up
What we'll re-evaluate, when, and based on what evidence.
```

## RFC vs ADR

- **RFC** ("Request for Comments") — proposal phase. Open-ended discussion.
- **ADR** — decision recorded after discussion ends.

An RFC may produce one or more ADRs. Some teams skip the RFC and go straight to ADR-as-proposal.

## Process

<Steps>
  <Step title="Draft">
    Author writes the ADR with status `Proposed`.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review">
    PR-style review. Comments on the markdown. Aim for resolution within 5 business days.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Decide">
    Tech lead or staff engineer accepts. Status flips to `Accepted`.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Communicate">
    Share in team channel. For cross-team impact, post in `#eng-architecture`.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Revisit">
    Per the follow-up section. Don't let ADRs go stale silently.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Superseded ADRs

When a new ADR replaces an old one:

- New ADR references the old one
- Old ADR's status changes to `Superseded by ADR-NNNN`
- Don't delete old ADRs — they're part of the institutional memory
