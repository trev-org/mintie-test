A runbook is the document an on-call uses at 3am. It should be specific enough to follow without your team's tribal knowledge.

## Where they live

Inside the service repo, at `docs/runbooks/<alert-name>.md`. They're auto-linked from the Service Catalog and from the alert payload itself — so when PagerDuty fires, the link to the runbook is in the SMS.

## Format

Every runbook follows this structure:

```markdown
# Alert: <alert-name>

## Symptoms
What the on-call sees. Dashboards, log queries, customer impact.

## Likely causes
Top 3-5 reasons this fires, ordered by frequency.

## Diagnostic steps
1. Step-by-step commands to confirm.
2. Use `intuit run <X>` rather than custom shell pipelines.

## Mitigation
What to do *now* to stop customer pain.

## Resolution
Longer-term fix path.

## Recent changes
Last 5 changes to this alert or service that might be related.

## Escalation
Who to page if mitigation fails.
```

## Quality bar

A runbook fails the bar if:

- It's a wall of text without commands or links
- It says "see so-and-so's notes"
- The first command is a 6-line shell pipeline
- It's been touched in less than 6 months but the service has changed
- It doesn't link back to the dashboards it references

## Examples

Strong examples to model:

- `intuit/payments/docs/runbooks/charge-decline-rate-spike.md`
- `intuit/identity/docs/runbooks/login-success-drop.md`
- `intuit/turbotax-efile/docs/runbooks/irs-submission-failures.md`

## Drills

Each team runs a **runbook drill** monthly:

1. Pick a runbook
2. New on-call follows it cold
3. Time how long until mitigation
4. Update anything unclear

## What's not a runbook

A runbook isn't:

- Architecture documentation (use `/docs/architecture/`)
- Deep-dive of failure modes (postmortems)
- General team docs

Keep them tight, specific, and actionable.
