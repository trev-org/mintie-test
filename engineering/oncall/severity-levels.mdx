Severity drives response speed, escalation, and visibility. Pick the right one quickly — we'd rather upgrade than downgrade.

## Definitions

| Severity | Customer impact                                     | Examples                                               | Initial response time |
| -------- | --------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------- |
| Sev-1    | Significant or total loss of service for many users | TurboTax e-file down on April 14; checkout broken      | 5 min ack, 15 min war room |
| Sev-2    | Meaningful degradation                              | Slow login (>5s p95); a region down                    | 15 min ack, 30 min eng on call |
| Sev-3    | Minor or partial impact                             | Edge feature broken; single-tenant issue               | Best-effort within hours       |
| Sev-4    | No customer impact, internal only                   | Dashboard broken; flaky test in CI                     | Next business day              |

Security severities (SEC-1 through SEC-4) follow [Incident response](/engineering/security/incident-response).

## Sev-1 specifics

Sev-1s are visible to the entire company:

- War room created automatically (Slack channel `#sev1-<short-tag>` and Zoom)
- Comms team notified — they handle external and internal messaging
- Status page updated within 30 min
- Executive page (CTO and product GM) within 30 min
- Customer support primed with FAQ
- Engineering leadership joins as observers

The **Incident Commander** for Sev-1s is typically a senior on-call engineer or SRE. The IC does not fix the bug — the IC drives the response.

## Sev-2 specifics

- War room recommended, not required
- Customer support proactively informed
- Status page updated if customer-facing
- Executive notified for sustained sev-2

## Sev-3 / Sev-4

- Tracked in Jira, no war room
- Resolved in normal sprint cadence

## Upgrades and downgrades

Sev levels can change as understanding improves. Document the change in the incident timeline. Common patterns:

- Starts as a sev-3, scope expands → upgrade to sev-2 or sev-1
- Starts as a sev-1 (assumed worst case), turns out narrow → downgrade to sev-2

Default to the higher severity when in doubt. The cost of a false sev-1 is a busy night; the cost of a missed sev-1 can be customers and dollars.
